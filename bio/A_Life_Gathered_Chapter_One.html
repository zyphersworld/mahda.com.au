PART ONE — THE MAKING

Chapter One — The Hands

There's a table in this caravan barely wide enough for a coffee cup and a notebook, and that's where I am most mornings now, in Koroit, with the kind of quiet I spent forty years too loud to hear. The kettle ticks. A magpie works the gum outside like it's being paid by the note. I'm sober now — not the way the chip-counters mean it, but in the older sense, the one the word actually carries before anyone narrowed it: clear, steady, in my right mind, off the things that were drowning me. Which after twenty years of not being is its own strange weather — like waking up in a body someone returned in better condition than you left it.

I've got my hands flat on the table. I do this sometimes. Look at them.

These are the hands that busked outside Target when I was eleven, case open on the footpath, a kid playing twelve-bar blues to people who mostly wanted me to move so they could get to the tinsel. They taught themselves the guitar the wrong way round at five — solved it backwards before anyone thought to set it up right — because the pull to play turned up years before the proper instrument did. They put a few holes in a police-station wall once. The wall lost. They wrote tens of thousands of lines of code over the years — back-office systems, a string of websites, and in the end a video game I gave away for nothing.

Same hands.

I used to need one of those things to not be true — the busker or the bloke at the wall, the builder or the wreck. Pick one, file the rest under not me, must've been a different fella, anyway. Took me a long time, and a lot of help, to land on the trick: you don't get to pick. They're all me. They all came out of the same set of hands. And sitting here now, hands flat on the wood, I've finally stopped pretending otherwise.

So we start where it started. Not with the harm — the harm came from outside, later, uninvited, and it does not get to go first. It's had enough of my time. We start with the making, because the making was here before any of it, and it's the only thing that never fully left.

And the making started at my mother's, on the floor, with a kitchen spatula.

There's a photograph of me at about two, sitting on my mother's lap in front of one of those painted studio backdrops they all had then — a lake, some autumn trees that never grew anywhere real. The whole thing's gone warm with age, sepia-tinted the way those old prints do. She's young in it, dark-haired, smiling like the camera caught her in the middle of something good. I'm a brown-blond scrap of a thing pressed against her, looking straight down the lens. On the back, in my own hand, decades later, I've written just two words. Mum and me.

I want that picture standing at the front of everything, because the music came from her, and I've always known it came from her, and to start anywhere else would be to start in the wrong place. Before the guitar, before the lessons, before any of it, there was my mother's record collection — four hundred albums, near enough, and she played them constantly, the house never quiet. That was the air I learned to breathe. A kid doesn't know he's being given anything. He just thinks the world comes with a soundtrack, and then he grows up and finds out most houses didn't.

And there was a stool. And a spatula. I'd kneel at that stool and drum on the seat of it with a spatula out of the kitchen drawer, banging along to the records — I can still name the one, Rocky Burnette, "Tired of Toein' the Line," that rockabilly thing from 1980 that would've been new when I was small. No instrument. No lessons. Nobody teaching me. Just a kid who couldn't not make the rhythm with his hands, using the only tool in reach, on the only surface available, because the pull was already in there and it didn't care what it had to use to get out.

That's the truest picture of me there is. Not a boy handed an instrument. A boy improvising one out of a kitchen and a stool, because the music was already trying to get out of him before anyone gave it anywhere to go.

And it was Mum who gave me the first real one. A ukulele. I was three. Too small for it, the way you always are for the first one. I don't remember the moment — nobody remembers being three with any honesty; what we've got is a feeling we've narrated over so many times it's worn smooth. But I know the shape of it, and the shape never changed: a small instrument, too big for me, and a grown-up making room for the sound. That's the whole template. Everything good that ever happened to me has that shape — someone making room, and sound coming out of it.

The ukulele was Mum's. The records were Mum's. The spatula on the stool was at Mum's. The guitar came later, at five, from someone else — a man you'll meet soon enough, who earned every bit of what I owe him. But the first instrument, and the whole record-soaked house it was played in, came from my mother. The deepest root of the only thing that never left me — that's hers, and I want it said first, and said plainly, before any of the rest.

By five I had a nylon-string guitar, and this is where Ray comes in — Ray Batten, the man who'd handed it to me, of whom there'll be much more later — and where it gets faintly ridiculous, because I taught myself to play it the wrong way round.

There's more of Ray to come, and it can wait. What matters here is just the shape of the thing: it was an ordinary right-handed guitar, strung the normal way, and I'm right-handed — but it felt more natural to me flipped over, held upside down, so that's how I played it. Which means the whole instrument is reversed: the low strings where the high ones should be, every chord shape inverted, the entire logic of the thing turned back to front. I didn't fix it. I just learned it like that — worked out Guitar Boogie, worked out twelve-bar blues, solved the whole upside-down puzzle of it, because the pull to play got there long before anyone could tell me I was doing it wrong. The pull doesn't care which way up the guitar is. It just wants out.

There's a family echo in that I didn't notice for years. My father was a natural left-hander at golf — stood to the ball on the left side, the way it came to him — except he did the opposite of me: he forced himself over to the orthodox right, drilled the swing onto the proper side and kept it there, and was good enough off it to play to a serious handicap regardless. Two ways to answer the same problem, his and mine. He took the way he was built and forced it into the standard shape and won that way. I took the standard shape and turned it upside down to suit the way I was built and won that way. Different directions, same stubbornness — the same refusal, running in the family, to do a thing the expected way just because it was expected, and the bloody-mindedness to be good enough that it didn't matter.

I've thought about this a lot, at that table, with my hands flat on it. That five-year-old took a thing that was the wrong way round and, instead of waiting for it to be fixed, learned to play it anyway. I'd love to tell you I only ever carried that as a triumph. Mostly I carried the other lesson it teaches a kid too: that the gear is going to come to you backwards, that no one's coming to set it up right, and that you'd best learn to make music on it as it is. You can hear how that lesson could save a life and wreck one. It did both. We'll get there.

But at five it was pure. No theory in it, no damage, just a small boy and a guitar set up wrong and the blues in E, which is still — forty years and everything in between later — my favourite thing to play. There's a twelve-bar in E that lives in my hands so deep I could be most of the way gone and still find it. I've tested this. Not on purpose. But I've tested it.

I should tell you what kind of world this was, so the gentleness reads at its true weight.

My father built things. Houses, first — he was a builder by trade, and a sharp businessman with it, and he built the very house we lived in with his own hands and lived in it until the bankruptcy of 1983 took everything. And it was the bankruptcy that took it, not him — the recession of those years came through and flattened better men than the timing let him be, and a couple of partners he should never have trusted helped it along. He didn't fail so much as get caught. And then he did the thing that tells you who he was: he rebuilt, and he rebuilt by building. He stopped building houses and he built a church instead — not attended one, built one, from nothing into something large and certain, with an answer ready for every question.

He came to it the long way. He'd been raised Catholic and had rejected the Catholic Church outright — would speak against it openly his whole life. He'd had years out in the ordinary world before any of this, and then he was pulled back, and the thing that pulled him was a television. My grandmother had Jimmy Swaggart preaching on the TV one day, and my father walked past it, and something in it caught him, and that was the hook. From there it was fast: a local Assemblies of God elder in Warrnambool, and then out on his own to build his own ministry. A televangelist on his mother's TV set is the seed the whole world I was raised in grew out of. Hold that. It comes back.

I spent most of my life reading the certainty of that world as cruelty. I was wrong about the mechanism, and it took me a long time to stop guessing at what drove him and just look at what was actually there: a builder who'd lost everything and rebuilt by conviction, who'd found a faith that gave him a structure to stand on and never let go of it. We were the church kids, the cult kids, depending on who was describing us and how kind they were feeling. And inside a world like that, a world organised around a single overwhelming answer, the people who just listen — who hand you an instrument and bend down to hear what you do with it, who don't need you to be saved or sorted or quiet — those people are the whole oxygen supply.

I want to be careful here, because it would be easy and cheap to make my father the villain of this story, and he isn't one. I used to think he was the source of the harm that found me. He wasn't. The harm came from somewhere else entirely — from a different world than my father's, a world he himself stood against, and I'll come to it. What my father did was make decisions, some of them with hard consequences he never intended — chief among them the day he pulled me out of one school and into another, and what that delivered me to. But the man had no ill intent. Naming the consequences is not the same as blaming him for the harm. I believe that now the way I believe my own name.

And he wasn't building in a vacuum. There were two of them, my parents, and they'd come apart when I was three and gone off in opposite directions — that's the part you can't understand a single one of us without. Around the time the floor fell out of his life, my father was reaching for the church, for structure, for a way up and out and better. My mother was on the other road entirely — still out with her friends, still surviving the ordinary way. The divorce hurt her badly, and she was, at times, a hurt and damaged and frankly unwell woman, and out of that hurt she said wrong things about my father to my brother and me, more than she should have. And he did the same in the other direction — put down her choices, her life, the road she'd taken. Two wounded people, each narrating the other to their sons through the worst of what they were feeling. The hard words came out of the damage, not out of who either of them was. And underneath my mother's, all the while, the records kept playing.

What it left me, being raised in the gap between their two accounts, was this: I wasn't raised inside one parent's certainty. I was raised in the space between two wounded people who couldn't agree on the most basic facts of who the other one was — and a kid who grows up there learns early that the people meant to hand him the truth can't give it to him whole, so he'd better go and find his own. Which, forty years on, is more or less what this table is for.

For now, the spatula and the stool. The four hundred records. The ukulele at three and the guitar at five. The making switched on early, before the world got its grip, which is the one piece of luck this whole story is built on. Whatever came later — and a lot came later — it could never quite get all the way down to that. It couldn't reach the toddler drumming on a stool with a spatula, or the three-year-old with the too-big ukulele, or the five-year-old playing the blues the wrong way round and grinning about it. Those three were sealed in before the damage arrived. Whatever the world did to me afterward, it never got them.

The hands were making things before anyone taught them to. They never really stopped.

Hold onto that. We're going to need it.
